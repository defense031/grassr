grassr calibration program · stage 4 post-mortem

The distribution was the object.

We tried to summarize the null behaviour of δ̂ — the cross-coefficient disagreement statistic — as a line of cut-offs: one threshold per design. The line came back U-shaped, cliff-ridden, and unstable. It wasn’t noise. Each point on that line was the extreme tail of a full distribution, and at small samples those distributions are mixtures — a calm, stable body plus a heavy contaminated tail that owns the 99th percentile. Below: the actual distributions — 18.4 million null panels drawn through the production pipeline itself — and the honest way to read a panel against them.

One statistic, five nulls

Every ridge is the null distribution of δ̂ at one panel quality q, for the design you pick — what disagreement between PABAK, AC1, and Fleiss’ κ looks like when nothing is wrong. Bin widths adapt to each ridge’s draw count, and the smooth-display toggle applies a light moving average to the drawn shape only — every percentile readout and cut is computed from raw draws. Comb-like structure that survives smoothing at high q and small N is the true discreteness of the null. The dot is the median; the amber and rust ticks are the 95th and 99th percentiles — the “cuts” a threshold table would ship — and the rust bar is the bootstrap uncertainty of that 99th percentile. The green line is your panel. Flip on the tail zoom to see what the cut-line was actually riding.

Why this is the honest convention

For the practitioner

Your Report Card already reads every coefficient as a position on its matched reference distribution — “46th percentile for a panel like yours.” The disagreement statistic now speaks the same language: “your coefficients disagree more than 97% of healthy panels at your design” is a sentence you can act on, and it degrades gracefully — a weird design gives you a blunt percentile, not a false verdict from a brittle cut-off.

For the statistician

The null of δ̂ at small N is a two-component mixture: a stable body (the median moves <2 pp across the whole quality range) and a degenerate-panel contamination whose mass, not location, drives extreme quantiles — hence non-monotone cut curves with bootstrap CIs 10 pp wide. Inverting an unstable quantile into a hard threshold manufactures decisions from tail noise. Evaluating the observed statistic’s ECDF position on the matched null is the stable dual: size control by construction at calibrated cells, uniform MC error, no line to draw through a mixture.

Fidelity check: resolved. These nulls are drawn through the production pipeline (check_asymmetry() per panel: real normalization, constant-rater dropping, clamp-guard). The production tails match the raw sweep almost exactly — the heavy small-N tails are real, not pipeline artifacts. Every ridge’s reported percentile is Monte-Carlo-stable to ≤0.5 pp (10k–100k draws per ridge). 41 ridges still carry an inversion-instability flag — their 99th-percentile value is not stably estimable (3 sit on true mass gaps) — which is precisely why the shipped convention reads percentiles and never inverts tails.
