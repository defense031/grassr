grassr calibration program · stage 4 post-mortem

The distribution was the object.

We tried to summarize the null behaviour of δ̂ — the cross-coefficient disagreement statistic — as a line of cut-offs: one threshold per design. The line came back U-shaped, cliff-ridden, and unstable. It wasn’t noise. Each point on that line was the extreme tail of a full distribution, and at small samples those distributions are mixtures — a calm, stable body plus a heavy contaminated tail that owns the 99th percentile. Below: the actual distributions, 13.2 million simulated panels’ worth, and the honest way to read a panel against them.

One statistic, five nulls

Every ridge is the null distribution of δ̂ at one panel quality q, for the design you pick — what disagreement between PABAK, AC1, and Fleiss’ κ looks like when nothing is wrong. The dot is the median; the amber and rust ticks are the 95th and 99th percentiles — the “cuts” a threshold table would ship — and the rust bar is the bootstrap uncertainty of that 99th percentile. The green line is your panel. Flip on the tail zoom to see what the cut-line was actually riding.

Why this is the honest convention

For the practitioner

Your Report Card already reads every coefficient as a position on its matched reference distribution — “46th percentile for a panel like yours.” The disagreement statistic now speaks the same language: “your coefficients disagree more than 97% of healthy panels at your design” is a sentence you can act on, and it degrades gracefully — a weird design gives you a blunt percentile, not a false verdict from a brittle cut-off.

For the statistician

The null of δ̂ at small N is a two-component mixture: a stable body (the median moves <2 pp across the whole quality range) and a degenerate-panel contamination whose mass, not location, drives extreme quantiles — hence non-monotone cut curves with bootstrap CIs 10 pp wide. Inverting an unstable quantile into a hard threshold manufactures decisions from tail noise. Evaluating the observed statistic’s ECDF position on the matched null is the stable dual: size control by construction at calibrated cells, uniform MC error, no line to draw through a mixture.

One open fidelity check, disclosed: these null draws were generated outside the production pipeline — in particular, without grass_report()’s dropping of constant raters. If the contaminated tail is mostly constant-rater panels, the production null is cleaner than what you see here, and the calibration will be re-derived through the production code path before anything ships. The tails above are therefore a worst case, which is the right direction to err.
