The GRASS Reporting Card: two-column tier labels

Preview vignette for the §8 Reporting Card of the merged GRASS binary- rater-reliability paper. The two functions exercised below — check_asymmetry() and classify() — ship with grass 0.2.x. Tier definitions follow paper2/review/framework_notes.md §0.6 (Column A, three-tier model safety) and §0.6.5 (“Labels without re-creating Landis-Koch”; Column B, five-tier use-case fit).

What a Reporting Card row looks like

Every panel reported under the GRASS framework carries two orthogonal tier columns alongside the arithmetic:

  • Column A (model safety) — is q̂ trustworthy as the primary summary? Assigned by check_asymmetry() from per-rater (Ŝe_j, Ŝp_j) estimates: ok (δ̂ < 0.05), caution (0.05–0.10), or unsafe (≥ 0.10). At Tier 3 the Se = Sp diagonal is visibly broken and the panel needs a latent-class fit rather than a single q̂.
  • Column B (use-case fit) — does the panel meet its declared use case? Assigned by classify() from the 95% CI on EMR_panel (the expected rate at which the panel’s majority-vote decision disagrees with the latent class) against a tolerance T drawn from the use-case ladder. Five tiers: Fails, Indeterminate, Meets, Met with distinction (upper CI below T/2), Exceeds (upper CI below the next harder use case’s tolerance).

The columns are independent: a panel can be ok + Met with distinction (the common case), caution + Meets (asymmetry-flagged but still passes), or unsafe + Exceeds (latent-class fit needed, but the panel already clears a harder use case once the fit is trusted).

Worked example 1: a screening panel, symmetric raters

Five radiologists independently score the same 50 chest films for a binary screening-eligibility endpoint. A simulation-calibrated latent- class fit gives per-rater sensitivity and specificity:

se <- c(R1 = 0.88, R2 = 0.86, R3 = 0.89, R4 = 0.87, R5 = 0.85)
sp <- c(R1 = 0.86, R2 = 0.87, R3 = 0.88, R4 = 0.85, R5 = 0.86)

Column A: is the Se = Sp diagonal defensible?

safety <- check_asymmetry(se = se, sp = sp, rater = names(se))
safety
#> grass asymmetry diagnostic (Column A of Reporting Card)
#>   raters              : 5
#>   per-rater gaps |Se-Sp|
#>     R1        Se = 0.880   Sp = 0.860   gap = 0.020
#>     R2        Se = 0.860   Sp = 0.870   gap = 0.010
#>     R3        Se = 0.890   Sp = 0.880   gap = 0.010
#>     R4        Se = 0.870   Sp = 0.850   gap = 0.020
#>     R5        Se = 0.850   Sp = 0.860   gap = 0.010
#>   delta_hat (max)    : 0.020
#>   thresholds           : caution = 0.050,  unsafe = 0.100
#>   tier                 : 1  (ok)
#>   reporting guidance   : Diagonal default: report q_hat plus EDR / EMR_panel.

The largest per-rater gap is 0.03, below the caution threshold of 0.05, so the panel sits at Tier 1 (ok). We may report q̂ ± SE plus EDR and EMR_panel without per-rater disclosure; PABAK’s prevalence-flatness assumption holds.

Column B: does the panel meet the screening use case?

Under Tier 1 the panel’s operating quality is q̂ ≈ 0.87 with SE ≈ 0.02 (Wilson-logit); the panel-level misclassification rate under 5-rater majority vote and its 95% CI are derived from q̂ and k:

q_hat <- 0.87
emr_point <- emr_panel(q = q_hat, k = 5)
# Rough 95% CI on EMR via propagating q_hat ± 1.96 * 0.02 through emr_panel()
emr_lo <- emr_panel(q = q_hat + 1.96 * 0.02, k = 5)  # lower EMR = upper q
emr_hi <- emr_panel(q = q_hat - 1.96 * 0.02, k = 5)  # upper EMR = lower q
c(point = emr_point, lower = emr_lo, upper = emr_hi)
#>       point       lower       upper 
#> 0.017908626 0.006503554 0.036977730

Hold the panel to the declared screening tolerance T = 0.10:

verdict <- classify(emr_upper = emr_hi, emr_lower = emr_lo,
                    emr_point = emr_point,
                    use_case = "screening",
                    regime = safety)
verdict
#> grass classification (Column B of Reporting Card)
#>   use case             : screening
#>   tolerance T          : 0.10   (Clinical screening (USPSTF-style))
#>   T_next (harder use)  : 0.05
#>   EMR_panel (point)    : 0.018
#>   EMR_panel 95% CI     : [0.007, 0.037]
#>   margin T - U         : +0.063
#>   tier                 : Exceeds
#>   Column A (model safe): tier 1  (ok, delta_hat = 0.020)
#>   action               : Proceed; consider promoting the panel's use case one step up the ladder.

Exceeds: the upper CI on EMR_panel (0.037) lies below the diagnostic tolerance T_next = 0.05, so the panel would also clear the next step up the ladder. Exceeds is the only tier that licenses promoting the panel’s declared use case — the label points to a specific operational consequence, not an adjective.

### The citation sentence

Every label must appear with its arithmetic — that is what keeps the label system citable without repeating the Landis-Koch error of stipulative, regime-free cutoffs:

“The rater panel exceeded the GRASS screening tier (q_hat = 0.87 +/- 0.02; EMR_panel = 0.018 at k = 5 majority vote, 95% CI [0.007, 0.037]; declared tolerance T = 0.10, margin 0.063; panel would also clear the diagnostic tolerance T_next = 0.05; inter-rater axis; N = 50; Tier 1 model safety per check_asymmetry() delta_hat = 0.020).”

Reads like a qualitative verdict; every adjective is reconstructible.

Worked example 2: smaller pilot, flagged asymmetry, harder use case

Now suppose the same five readers have been re-fielded for a diagnostic use case (tolerance T = 0.05). Two changes: the calibration pilot is only N = 20 films (so SE on q̂ is roughly doubled to 0.04), and one rater has drifted into a visible Se-favoring asymmetry (a known quirk of the reading protocol when on shift > 10 hours).

se2 <- c(R1 = 0.88, R2 = 0.86, R3 = 0.93, R4 = 0.87, R5 = 0.85)
sp2 <- c(R1 = 0.86, R2 = 0.87, R3 = 0.84, R4 = 0.85, R5 = 0.86)

safety2 <- check_asymmetry(se = se2, sp = sp2, rater = names(se2))
safety2
#> grass asymmetry diagnostic (Column A of Reporting Card)
#>   raters              : 5
#>   per-rater gaps |Se-Sp|
#>     R1        Se = 0.880   Sp = 0.860   gap = 0.020
#>     R2        Se = 0.860   Sp = 0.870   gap = 0.010
#>     R3        Se = 0.930   Sp = 0.840   gap = 0.090
#>     R4        Se = 0.870   Sp = 0.850   gap = 0.020
#>     R5        Se = 0.850   Sp = 0.860   gap = 0.010
#>   delta_hat (max)    : 0.090
#>   thresholds           : caution = 0.050,  unsafe = 0.100
#>   tier                 : 2  (caution)
#>   reporting guidance   : Diagonal + diagnostic: report q_hat with caution flag and delta_hat.

R3’s gap of 0.09 is below the unsafe threshold but above caution, so the panel sits at Tier 2. q̂ remains reportable as the primary summary but must carry a caution flag and the δ̂ value; R3’s asymmetry is disclosed so readers can weigh it.

# Wider CI from the smaller pilot
emr_lo2 <- emr_panel(q = q_hat + 1.96 * 0.04, k = 5)
emr_hi2 <- emr_panel(q = q_hat - 1.96 * 0.04, k = 5)
c(lower = emr_lo2, upper = emr_hi2)
#>       lower       upper 
#> 0.001269737 0.064574605

verdict2 <- classify(emr_upper = emr_hi2, emr_lower = emr_lo2,
                     emr_point = emr_point,
                     use_case = "diagnostic",
                     regime = safety2)
verdict2
#> grass classification (Column B of Reporting Card)
#>   use case             : diagnostic
#>   tolerance T          : 0.05   (Clinical test validation (CLSI-style))
#>   T_next (harder use)  : 0.02
#>   EMR_panel (point)    : 0.018
#>   EMR_panel 95% CI     : [0.001, 0.065]
#>   margin T - U         : -0.015
#>   tier                 : Indeterminate
#>   Column A (model safe): tier 2  (caution, delta_hat = 0.090)
#>   action               : Collect more data (increase N) or add raters (increase k).

Indeterminate: the 95% CI on EMR_panel straddles T = 0.05 — the point estimate is good, but the pilot is too thin to resolve whether the panel is below or above the diagnostic tolerance. The operational action is not “fail” — it is “collect more data”. Either more subjects (tighten the CI) or more raters (raise k, drop EMR_panel). The same panel that Exceeds the screening tolerance can be honestly called Indeterminate for diagnostic use; that is exactly the regime-conditioned behaviour the Landis-Koch scheme cannot express.

Assembling a two-row Reporting Card

Both verdicts bind cleanly into a single results table:

card <- rbind(
  cbind(row_label = "Screening (N = 50)", as.data.frame(verdict)),
  cbind(row_label = "Diagnostic pilot (N = 20)", as.data.frame(verdict2))
)
card[, c("row_label", "use_case", "tolerance", "emr_upper",
         "margin", "tier", "column_a_tier", "column_a_regime")]
#>                   row_label   use_case tolerance  emr_upper      margin
#> 1        Screening (N = 50)  screening      0.10 0.03697773  0.06302227
#> 2 Diagnostic pilot (N = 20) diagnostic      0.05 0.06457461 -0.01457461
#>            tier column_a_tier column_a_regime
#> 1       Exceeds             1              ok
#> 2 Indeterminate             2         caution

That data.frame is the literal object the §8 Reporting Card renders in the paper.

What the framework commits to

  1. Labels live on EMR_panel, not on any individual agreement coefficient. κ / PABAK / AC1 / α / ICC all feed q̂ upstream; q̂ and k feed EMR_panel; only EMR_panel’s CI against T assigns the use-case tier.
  2. T is declared, not stipulated, and comes from use-case precedent (see grass_use_case_ladder()) or from the practitioner with explicit disclosure.
  3. “With distinction” means margin within the declared use case; “Exceeds” means the panel would pass a harder use case. The distinction is load-bearing, not cosmetic — only Exceeds licenses promoting the panel one step up the ladder.
  4. Every label appears with its arithmetic. A reviewer who asks “with distinction under what conditions?” finds the answer in the parenthetical.
